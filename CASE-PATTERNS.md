# Recognized Clinical Patterns

This document describes recurrent patterns of clinical, imaging, CSF,
and disease-course findings that, in our experience and in the
literature, are at high risk of being labeled "idiopathic" before all
accessible diagnostic possibilities have been exhausted.

Each pattern includes pointers to the differential entries most
relevant to that pattern.

---

## Pattern 1: Rapidly progressive symmetric corticospinal–cerebellar syndrome with intrathecal IgG synthesis, refractory to first-line immunotherapy

### Description

A patient in the third or fourth decade of life develops, over months
to a few years, a combination of:

- Cerebellar ataxia (gait, appendicular, dysarthria), often asymmetric
  initially and becoming progressive and disabling
- Pyramidal signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia), frequently
  left-predominant or asymmetric early, with progression toward
  bilateral involvement
- Bilateral and symmetric T2/FLAIR hyperintensity along the
  corticospinal tracts, extending from the cerebral peduncles into
  the subcortical white matter
- Mild cerebellar atrophy as a secondary finding
- CSF with high oligoclonal band count (often >10), without typical
  MS-pattern lesion distribution on MRI
- Failure to respond to high-dose corticosteroids and IVIg
- Eventual stabilization or "burn-out" of the active inflammatory
  picture over years, leaving structural sequelae and atrophy

### Typical workup gaps

In presentations matching this pattern, the following tests are
sometimes omitted, particularly outside specialized centers:

- **HTLV-1 serology** (and CSF HTLV-1 PCR if available) —
  regionally relevant in South America, parts of the Caribbean,
  sub-Saharan Africa, and southern Japan. HAM/TSP can present with
  corticospinal predominance, intrathecal IgG, and oligoclonal bands.
  *See `DIFFERENTIAL/infectious/HTLV-1.md` (planned).*

- **Very long chain fatty acids (VLCFA) in plasma** — to screen for
  adult-onset X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy / adrenomyeloneuropathy.
  The cerebro-cerebellar variant of adult X-ALD can present with
  spastic-ataxic syndromes and bilateral CST involvement. Implications
  extend to first-degree female relatives (X-linked carriers).
  *See `DIFFERENTIAL/metabolic/X-ALD.md` (planned).*

- **SCA27B genetic testing** — late-onset cerebellar ataxia due to GAA
  expansion in *FGF14* intron 1, first described in 2023. May not be
  included in older ataxia panels.
  *See `DIFFERENTIAL/genetic/SCA27B.md`.*

- **Expanded adult leukoencephalopathy panel** — CSF1R, GFAP (adult-
  onset Alexander), HSP genes (SPG-related), particularly when imaging
  shows symmetric white matter findings.
  *See `DIFFERENTIAL/genetic/adult-leukoencephalopathies.md` (planned).*

- **Copper and ceruloplasmin** — Wilson disease can present in this
  age range; cheap, accessible test.

- **CSF EBV DNA quantification** — not part of standard practice but
  may be informative in light of the hypothesis under investigation
  (see `HYPOTHESES/EBV-FGF14-silencing.md`).

### What this pattern is not

- It is not typical multiple sclerosis. Lesion distribution
  (symmetric, CST-centered) and treatment response differ.
- It is not typical NMOSD (negative AQP4 and MOG antibodies expected).
- It is not necessarily inflammatory in the autoimmune sense — the
  failure of MP/IVIg suggests either a non-immune mechanism or an
  immune mechanism not captured by current first-line therapies.

### Clinical course note

The eventual stabilization of the active process (normalization of
CRP, transaminases, ESR; resolution of T2 hyperintensity in CST on
follow-up imaging; appearance of residual signal changes and mild
atrophy) is part of the natural history of this pattern. It does not
imply that the original etiology has been resolved; it implies that
the active phase has ended, with structural sequelae.

### Why this pattern matters

Time to diagnosis matters less for cure (often there is no cure) and
more for **what the family understands and what relatives can be
offered**. An identified etiology (genetic, infectious, metabolic)
gives families a name for what happened; offers, when applicable,
genetic counseling and predictive testing for relatives; and adds a
case to the literature that may help the next patient.

The absence of a name leaves a family with an unnamed loss and the
impression that medicine simply did not try hard enough — even when
extensive workup was performed — because the specific accessible
tests for the rarer entities were not requested.
