# SCA27B (GAA-FGF14 Ataxia)

**Category:** Genetic (autosomal dominant, late onset)
**OMIM:** 620174

## Overview

Spinocerebellar ataxia 27B is caused by an autosomal dominant
expansion of a GAA repeat in intron 1 of *FGF14*. Identified as a
disease entity in 2023, it has rapidly emerged as one of the most
common genetic causes of previously idiopathic late-onset cerebellar
ataxia. In several published cohorts of unsolved adult-onset cerebellar
ataxia, SCA27B accounts for between 10% and 60% of cases, depending on
the cohort and ascertainment criteria.

The molecular consequence of the GAA expansion is reduced *FGF14*
expression in cerebellum. Epigenetic silencing analogous to that seen
with *FXN* in Friedreich ataxia has been proposed as a contributory
mechanism, although the precise pathway remains under investigation.

## Clinical features

- Late onset (typically fifth decade or later, with wide range)
- Slowly progressive cerebellar syndrome: gait ataxia, dysarthria,
  appendicular incoordination
- **Downbeat nystagmus** is characteristic and a strong clinical clue
- Frequent **episodic component** (transient worsening triggered by
  exertion, alcohol, fatigue, or unclear stimuli) — this episodic
  feature is one of the most useful clinical pointers
- May occur with or without family history; reduced penetrance and
  variable expressivity are reported

## Imaging

- Cerebellar atrophy (variable, may be subtle or absent early)
- No specific signature distinguishing from other late-onset
  cerebellar atrophies on standard imaging

## Diagnostic test

**Targeted genotyping of the GAA-*FGF14* repeat**, typically by
long-range PCR or repeat-primed PCR, with sizing of the expansion.

Pathogenic threshold has been refined since the initial description;
current consensus generally considers expansions ≥250 GAA repeats as
pathogenic, with intermediate ranges (~200–250) considered
uncertain or reduced-penetrance.

### Availability

Coverage of SCA27B has been added to many commercial ataxia panels
since 2024. **Older panels (pre-2024) may not include this gene.**
When ordering an ataxia panel, confirm explicitly that the GAA-*FGF14*
expansion is included; standard exome sequencing **does not detect**
this intronic repeat expansion.

Standalone GAA-*FGF14* testing is available through several specialty
labs internationally, including some that offer the test as a
reduced-cost or research collaboration. Updated lists of providers
should be maintained in a `LABS/` directory (planned).

## Treatment-relevant note

**4-aminopyridine (4-AP)** has shown symptomatic benefit in
GAA-*FGF14* ataxia in published cohorts, including n-of-1 trials,
with reduction in episodic symptoms and modest improvement in steady-
state ataxia in some patients (Pellerin et al. 2023, Wirth et al.
2023). Use is supportive rather than disease-modifying.

This information is included for completeness; treatment decisions
remain the responsibility of treating physicians.

## Why this entry exists

Because SCA27B was identified after the design of many older ataxia
panels and most ataxia clinical pathways, patients presenting between
2023 and the present may have had ataxia panels that did not include
this gene. Adding SCA27B testing — or repeat-targeted testing — is one
of the highest-yield additions to the workup of unsolved late-onset
cerebellar ataxia at the time of writing.

## Differential considerations

- SCA27A (allelic) — caused by point mutations, deletions, or
  microdeletions in *FGF14*, with earlier onset and often cognitive/
  developmental features. Distinct from SCA27B in clinical course.
- Other late-onset SCAs (SCA6, SCA8, SCA11, etc.) — clinical overlap;
  panel testing recommended.
- See `CASE-PATTERNS.md` for the broader differential when SCA27B is
  negative and presentation is atypical.
